#### Energy density and flux

The energy density of electromagnetic waves is the energy per unit volume, which is a function of position and time $w=w(\boldsymbol{x},t)$: 

$$w = \frac{1}{2}(\varepsilon_0E^2+\frac{B^2}{\mu_0})\quad \textrm{(in vacuum)}$$
$$w = \frac{1}{2}(\varepsilon E^2+\frac{B^2}{\mu_0})\quad \textrm{(in linear isotropic medium)}$$
$$\bar w = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon E_0^2=\frac{B_0^2}{2\mu}\quad \textrm{(For plane wave with amplitude $E_0$ or $B_0$ in linear isotropic medium)}$$

The energy flux is a vector describing the energy flow per unit time per unit area, and is also called **Poynting vector**:

$$\boldsymbol{S}=\boldsymbol{E}\times\boldsymbol{H} \quad \textrm{(universally applicable)}$$
$$\boldsymbol{\bar S}=\frac{1}{2}\sqrt\frac{\varepsilon}{\mu}E_0^2\boldsymbol{e}_k = \frac{1}{2}v\varepsilon E_0^2\boldsymbol{e}_k \quad \textrm{(For plane wave in linear isotropic medium)}$$
For a laser in vacuum, the magnitude of $\boldsymbol{\bar S}$ is also the definition of laser intensity $I=\frac{1}{2}c\varepsilon_0 E_0^2$.
